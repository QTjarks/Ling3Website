Assignment 4.1: Language in theory

The goal of this exercise was to study the history of language methodology and make a summary of at least 5 theories which 12 to 16 year old students need to be able to understand.

Theory one: the grammar translating method

The first theory on how language was taught has to do with direct translation. In the west at the 17th, 18th and 19th century languages like Latin and Greek were considered to be foreign languages. These languages were taught mostly for the people speaking them to sound more intellectual. Within these languages, grammatical rules were of significant importance together with things like memorization of vocabulary and translating texts. Back then, there was no real need to be taught to speak the language, the only real purpose being to sound scholarly. In the late 19th century, the classic method came to be known as the “Grammar translation method”, which still did not offer much else than the grammatical rules when translating a text from a second language to the native one. Even now, this method is still widely and prominently used to teach students a new language and has survived multiple reforms of the teaching methods. In the end, this method only teaches the student to dissect a sentence into different parts to translate it into their native language and will not actually improve a students’ communication skills. (Brown, 2002)

Theory two: Audiolingual method

This theory has also been named the “army method” as it started in the army without any special programs. During the second world war, the soldiers figured that being able to understand the language their enemies were speaking would give them an advantage in battle, so they set out to teach themselves those languages. The focus of the audiolingual method is not on perfectly understanding words, rather it was focussed on understanding structures and patterns that would show in normal conversations. This way, they could quickly catch onto anything their enemies were saying. This method tries to make sure the pronunciation is correct, and an immediate response can be made. The learning of reading and writing was dependent on how well the person pronounced the language as they did not have any source material to go off of. (UK, 2018)
Theory three: Communicative language learning

This method was developed while the revolution was taking place in British teaching methods in the late 1960’s. Before, students were taught a language by hearing a language from their surroundings. This method was developed as a complete opposite of the previously mentioned audiolingual method (which focusses on memorisation). This method focusses on developing the students’ ability by having the students practice in real life situations instead of a textbook, focussing more on meaning than the accuracy and correctness of the sentence. (UK, 2018)
Theory four: Task-based language learning

This method shifted the focus from learning directly from a textbook to a more practical approach, a problem solving one. This method is formed from the belief that learners should be given tasks to complete, all the while learning the language. The goal of this method would be to give the students a task to complete while all communication during the solving of that task would have to be in the English language. This way, the students would learn to speak fluently and effectively. (Richards, 2001)
Theory five: Suggestopedia

Instead of actively improving or learning the active language, a method was developed to learn languages passively. Lozanov believed that we are able to learn much more than we think. Based on Soviet research he came up with a learning method that used relaxation as a way to pick up new knowledge. Naturally, music was a very important part of this teaching method. The way this method would work is that while the students were relaxed, readings, role-plays and dramas would be played in the background to get the students relaxed enough to be able to pick up this knowledge without having to focus on it.
