Language Myth 11

“Italian is beautiful, German is ugly.” By Howard Giles and Nancy Niedzielski

The aesthetic of a language is a commonly discussed topic because of how vague the matter really is. Languages that sound sophisticated and elegant are often preferred over languages that may come off as rude or uncultured.

The “beauty” of a language can even differ per accent within that language, where for example a London Cockney accent can be considered quite brash per occasion.
One theory on why a language can be considered beautiful is that some languages inherently sound better. That the human mind is biologically hardwired to be appealed to a certain language, be it grammatical structure or tones used in speech.

A different theory is that finding a particular language beautiful depends on the native tongue of the practitioner. When a person interacts with a language often, they will see it as a norm to which all other languages will have to compete. Which is why someone who originates from Saudi-Arabia might consider harsher tones to be elegant, which might be considered brash or rude to individuals who come from different countries.

I find that the theory about the native tongue has the most merit based on the different outlooks different individuals have when their country of origin differs. I also think that a foreign language sounds ‘mysterious’ to many. You can compare it to the saying of ‘People with straight hair want curly hair, people with curly hair want straight hair.” Because when I still worked as a waitress, I once served two Frenchmen who told me that ‘my language was so beautiful and that they’d love to learn it someday.
While, to me, I would definitely prefer French over Dutch. Perhaps even the most beautiful language becomes dull when you have spoken it for many years.
